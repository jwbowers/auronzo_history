Province of Belluno · Veneto · the Dolomites

Auronzo
di Cadore

Aurònzo · Aurònze — in Ladin

A valley on the river Ansiei, beneath the Tre Cime di Lavaredo.

The people of this valley have been Veneti, subjects of a patriarch, citizens of their own mountain community, Venetians, Austrians — and, only since 1866, Italians. For most of that long time they governed themselves and lived from their pastures, their forests, and the ore under their mountains. This is that story, told from the rock up: who ruled the valley, how it decided things, and how its people earned their bread.

Auronzo bella al piano stendentesi lunga tra l'acque, sotto la fosca Ajàrnola. — Giosuè Carducci, Cadore

For the descendants of Auronzo, gathered again.

The rock & the icebefore 8000 BCE

The valley before anyone

Start with the rock. The pale towers above Auronzo — the Tre Cime, the Marmarole, the Ajarnola — began as a coral reef in a warm, shallow sea about 250 million years ago. Over immense time the reef hardened into dolomite, the old seabed buckled upward, and the peaks were lifted into the shapes you see. The Dolomites are a fossil of that sea, and a UNESCO World Heritage site.

The last Ice Age did the final carving. Until roughly twelve thousand years ago glaciers filled these valleys; as they drew back they ground out the broad trough of the Ansiei, left the lakes, and uncovered a land that — for the first time — people could enter.

The first people~8000 BCE

Hunters in the high pastures

No one owned this land yet, and no one ruled it. The first people here were hunters who climbed up in summer after red deer, ibex, and chamois, and went back down before winter. They carried flint from distant sources to knap their blades — proof that even then these mountains lay on routes of exchange, not at the edge of the world.

The clearest trace is just over the ridge, at Mondeval above San Vito di Cadore: the grave of a hunter who died around 7,500 years ago, laid under a great boulder with his tools and ornaments. He is among the oldest people ever found high in the Alps, and he tells us the valleys around Auronzo were lived in long before any town, any lord, or any state.

Mondeval lies in neighbouring San Vito di Cadore, not Auronzo itself — but it is the closest window we have onto who first used these high valleys.

Veneti & Catubrini~600–200 BCE

A tribe, a herd, a holy place

By the Iron Age the valley belonged to the world of the Veneti, the people of the plain to the south, and to a mountain tribe the Romans later named the Catubrini — a name built from old words for battle and fortress. Cadore takes its name from them.

These were not cut-off herders. The Veneti had their own alphabet, and they met at sanctuaries that served as gathering place, court, and market at once. The great one for Cadore stood at Lagole, near today's Calalzo, where for centuries people left inscribed bronze offerings. Authority here was not a king or a capital. It was the clan, the herd, and the shared holy ground.

Who ruled
No central power — tribal and clan-based, with sanctuaries as common institutions.
Living from
Herding, metalwork, and trade across the Alpine passes between the Veneto plain and the north.
In their own words · Venetic, c. 400–100 BCE

eik goltanos doto louderai kanei

"Goltanos gave this to the goddess Loudera Kanis."

A dedication scratched on a bronze vessel found in Cadore. The script: the Veneti learned writing from the Etruscans around 600 BCE and were the most literate people of the pre-Roman Alps. Their alphabet ran right to left and used a strange "syllabic punctuation" that bracketed certain letters with dots — the word for gave was written dona.s.to. The deity's exact name is still argued over.

Lagole is in Calalzo, a short way down-valley; its bronzes now sit in the archaeological museum at Pieve di Cadore.

Under Rome2nd c BCE – 5th c CE

Rome ruled, but from far off

Rome absorbed the region into its province of Venetia, yet its grip on these mountains was light. Life went on much as before — and so did the old tongue. On Monte Calvario, the hill behind Villagrande in Auronzo itself, people kept writing in the Venetic alphabet into the first century CE, long after the plains had gone over to Latin.

That hill held a stepped sanctuary, in use from before the Roman conquest until the fourth or fifth century, where offerings went to a local god, the Maistratores. People earned their living as they long had: herding, cutting timber, and digging lead and silver from the rock. The empire taxed and defended from a distance; the valley handled its own daily affairs.

Found in Auronzo

The sanctuary on Monte Calvario, behind the cemetery at Villagrande, was rediscovered by chance in the 1990s. Its inscriptions are some of the latest Venetic writing known — a sign that here, the old culture outlived the Roman conquest by generations.

Who ruled
Rome, through provincial structures — present but remote in the high valleys.
Living from
Pasture, timber, and the first organised mining of lead and silver; trade over the passes.
In their own words · Latin, Roman era

APOLLINI

"To Apollo."

When Rome came, the healing power once called Trumusiate was worshipped here as Apollo. Real Latin dedications to Apollo turn up among the offerings at the Lagole sanctuary in Cadore — cut on statuette bases and on the handles of the bronze ladles — and the community itself, the teuta, left public dedications there too. The script: Roman square capitals, words split by dots, no lowercase and no spaces — the alphabet we still use. The fuller texts survive in the Lagole corpus; this is the dedicatory form they take.

The Patriarch's land5th c – 1335

A faraway church-prince, a local lord, and the villages underneath

When Rome fell, the faith stayed and the structure changed. Christianity reached Cadore from the Patriarchate of Aquileia, the great church-state of Friuli; Pieve di Cadore became the mother parish — its name, pieve, means exactly that. In 1077 the Emperor granted the Patriarch of Aquileia worldly power, and Cadore became, in law, the Patriarch's land.

Day to day, a distant church-prince was barely a ruler. The Patriarch held Cadore through a noble family, the da Camino, who governed it as his vassals and collected its dues until 1335. Beneath that feudal arrangement the villages were already running themselves: each had its Regola, an assembly of the founding households that owned the woods, pastures, and water in common and decided their use together. The commons came first; the lord sat on top of them.

Who ruled
The Patriarch of Aquileia in name; the da Camino family in practice; the village Regola over the local commons.
Living from
Subsistence farming and herding, forest, and small-scale mining — much of it on land held collectively.
In their own words · Latin, 1077

Heinricus divina favente clementia Romanorum rex

"Henry, by the favour of God's mercy, king of the Romans —"

The opening style of the imperial diplomas of Henry IV, whose 1077 grant at Pavia made the Patriarch lord of Cadore. Not Greek: though Aquileia had old ties to the Byzantine East, by 1077 it was a Latin-rite principality of the Holy Roman Empire, and Latin was the language of its law and its Church. The Roman tongue had simply become medieval, written now in a rounded Carolingian hand on parchment.

The Statutes of 13381338–1420

A community of villages writes its own law

In 1335 the last da Camino lord died suddenly, and the villages took their chance. In 1338 they put it in writing: the Statutes of Cadore, a charter that joined twenty-seven village Regole into one self-governing body — the Magnifica Comunità di Cadore. This is the institution behind "all those Cadore towns." Auronzo was one member village; Pieve di Cadore was the capital, where the council sat.

How it worked was strikingly modern. The twenty-seven Regole were grouped into ten districts, the Centenari. Each district elected and swore in two representatives to a "Greater and General Council." Serving was compulsory — miss a session without good cause and you were fined. The Patriarch stayed the nominal sovereign and recognised the charter in 1347, but from now on the valley made its own laws and ran its own affairs. It would keep doing so for nearly five hundred years.

Why so many town names

Cadore was a federation, not a single town. When this history keeps naming Pieve, Lozzo, Vigo, Domegge, San Vito — and Auronzo — it is naming member villages of one community. Long before modern states, these mountain villages had worked out, in their own way, how to own and run a forest together.

Who ruled
The valley itself — an elected Greater and General Council under written statutes, with the Patriarch as distant overlord.
Living from
Common pastures and forests managed by the Regole; herding, timber, and mining.
In their own words · Latin & Cadorino, 1338

latinus grossus

"thick Latin" — the working Latin of the local notaries

The Statutes were written in this rough chancery Latin. Whenever the law had to name a local tool, a custom, or a field, the notary reached for the valley's own speech and slipped a Cadorino word into the Latin. Those embedded words are the oldest written traces of Ladin in Cadore — the language was spoken for centuries before anyone wrote it down. The original parchment was carried off as plunder in 1511 and still sits in Innsbruck.

Under Venice1420–1797

The valley chooses a protector

By 1420 the Patriarchate was collapsing, and Cadore picked a new protector: the Republic of Venice. The choice was deliberate and the terms were easy. Venice took sovereignty but let the Magnifica Comunità keep its statutes and self-rule; a Venetian official sat above the council without replacing it.

What Venice wanted was wood. Cadore's forests became a strategic supply for the Venetian Arsenal — beams, oars, and ship timber — floated down the Piave on rafts, the zattere. Lead, zinc, and silver came out of the Argentiera mine up the Ansiei. And the valley sent Venice something else: a boy from Pieve di Cadore named Tiziano Vecellio — Titian — who became the greatest painter of the Republic. For three and a half centuries Auronzo lived under the winged lion, comfortable by mountain standards and still largely its own master.

Who ruled
Venice held sovereignty; the Magnifica Comunità kept its statutes and ran the valley's internal life.
Living from
Timber for the Venetian fleet, floated down the Piave; the Argentiera lead-zinc-silver mine; pasture and wool.
In their own words · Latin, 1420

Eamus ad bonos Venetos!

"Let us go to the good Venetians!"

The cry, in Latin, with which the Cadore council is said to have voted in 1420 to put the valley under Venice, recorded two centuries later by the historian Giuseppe Ciani. Under Venice the written language of business began shifting from Latin toward the Venetian and Italian vernacular — though in the valley's own mouths the everyday speech stayed Ladin.

Empires & borders1797–1866

The self-governing valley is abolished

The end came from outside. In 1797 Napoleon handed the dying Republic of Venice to Austria; a few years later the same lands passed to his own Kingdom of Italy. In 1807 his administrators did what five centuries had not: they abolished the Magnifica Comunità and the Regole and replaced them with modern municipalities — nineteen comuni, grouped into the cantons of Auronzo and Pieve. The valley's old self-government was gone, and the common lands were handed to the new town councils.

After Napoleon fell, Austria took the region back and held it, as part of the Kingdom of Lombardy-Venetia, until 1866. For the people of Auronzo this run of empires and redrawn borders mattered less for which flag flew than for what had quietly been lost beneath it: the right to govern themselves by their own law.

The real break

If you are looking for the moment the old Cadore ended, it is 1807 — not the Italian unification that came later. Napoleon's pen, not Italy's, closed the five-hundred-year republic of villages.

Into Italy — and out into the world1866–1914

Becoming Italian, and leaving

On 21 October 1866 a plebiscite joined the Veneto, and with it the main body of Cadore, to the Kingdom of Italy; in Auronzo the first council under Italian law met that same month, once the Austrian troops had withdrawn. "Becoming Italian" is this recent. The Magnifica Comunità was revived in 1875 — but only as a cultural body now, keeper of the valley's memory, not its government.

When did Auronzo become Italian?

In 1866 — but not all of Ladinia joined with it. The new border ran along the peaks just north of the town, and the Ladin communities a few valleys over — Cortina d'Ampezzo, Livinallongo, Colle Santa Lucia — stayed Austrian for another fifty years. They became Italian only after the First World War, by the Treaty of Saint-Germain (1919) and the transfer of 1920. The frontier cut straight through Ladin country, with Auronzo pressed against its southern edge. That is why, in 1915, the war opened on Auronzo's own mountains.

Italy did not make the mountains richer. The land was poor, the families large, and work was scarce. So Auronzo did what much of Cadore did: it left. Through the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries its people went out as woodcutters, miners, and stonemasons, and above all as gelatieri — ice-cream makers who opened shops across Austria, Germany, the Netherlands, and on to the Americas, with Argentina a common destination. They sent money home and came back in summer. While the men were gone — in the forests all winter, behind a counter abroad for the season — the farms, the animals, and the children were the women's to keep. The reunion this site was made for is part of that history: a family scattered by the same hard valley it still calls home.

Who ruled
The Kingdom of Italy, through ordinary comuni; the Magnifica survived only as a heritage body.
Living from
A thin mountain economy of mining and timber — and, increasingly, wages sent home by emigrants abroad.

The gelatieri story belongs to Cadore and the wider Belluno mountains as a whole; Auronzo shared fully in it.

The mountains at war1915–1918

A front line on the peaks

From 1915 to 1918 the border running along the peaks above Auronzo became a front. Italy and Austria-Hungary fought across the Dolomites, and the valley turned into a vast supply base — roads, depots, and mule tracks built to feed the fighting on Monte Piana, the Tre Cime, and Forcella Giralba.

On Monte Piana alone, over two years, roughly fourteen thousand men died for a flat-topped hill that changed hands almost not at all. The first to fall, hours after war was declared on 24 May 1915, were two alpini of the Pieve di Cadore battalion — local men. The trenches and rock tunnels are still there, now an open-air museum. It is the nearest this self-governing, hard-working valley ever came to being destroyed.

Between the wars1918–1943

The valley under Mussolini

After the war came the regime, and with it two changes to the valley. In 1932 a dam across the Ansiei drowned the valley floor to make the lake of Santa Caterina — built for hydroelectric power, it became the centre of a tourism the Fascist state encouraged. The state also found a use for the killing-grounds above the town: it organised pilgrimages up to Monte Piana and the Tre Cime, sending veterans, families, and schoolchildren to venerate the war dead as a national rite.

Sanctions after the 1935 invasion of Ethiopia pushed Italy onto its own resources, and the old lead and zinc mines drew fresh interest. The mountains were soon armed again, too. When Hitler annexed Austria in 1938, Mussolini's Germany now sat just over the northern ridge — and Mussolini did not trust it. Between 1939 and 1942 his engineers cut tunnels and gun positions into the rock at Cima Gogna, the gateway to the Ansiei valley, part of a fortified line aimed at the ally to the north. The guns were never fully installed.

Linea non mi fido

The people of Auronzo had a name for the fortifications Mussolini was building against his own German ally: the "Line I don't trust." They read the alliance more clearly than the regime did.

Occupation & Resistance1943–1945

Foreign rule, and the fight against it

When Italy surrendered in September 1943, Belluno did not pass to Mussolini's puppet republic. Germany took the province directly, folding it into a zone run from Tyrol and meant for the Reich. For a valley that had been securely Italian only since 1866, German rule felt like foreign occupation — and the resistance to it was hard.

Belluno raised one of Italy's strongest partisan movements. Fighters of the Garibaldi division named for Nino Nannetti, and the Calvi brigade in Cadore and Comelico, held the high villages and the mountains while the Germans held the valley floor and the larger towns from Auronzo to Cortina. The reprisals were brutal. Women carried messages, food, and weapons through the clandestine women's defence groups — the valley's work again falling, as it always had, to those who stayed.

The price, across the province

When Belluno received Italy's gold medal for military valour in 1947, the citation counted the cost: 86 hanged, 277 shot, 7 burned alive, 11 dead under torture, 564 fallen in combat, 1,667 deported, 7,000 interned.

The valley empties1945–1970s

Turin, Fiat, and the long leaving

Peace did not stop the leaving; it changed where people went. Through the 1950s and 1960s, while northern Italy boomed, the mountains drained toward the factory cities — Milan, and above all Turin and Fiat. For a young person in Auronzo the choice was stark: stay for a thinning life of pasture, small workshops, and seasonal work, or take a wage on an assembly line in a city. Many took the wage. Auronzo had held around 4,500 people in the late 1800s; over the following century it lost roughly a third.

Then, just down the valley, came the decade's catastrophe. On the night of 9 October 1963 a mountainside slid into the reservoir behind the new Vajont dam and threw a wave over the top that erased the town of Longarone and killed about 1,900 people. The dam itself stood: the disaster was made by the men who had built a reservoir against the warnings of the rock. Longarone was the Piave's down-valley heart — home of the timber rafts and of many emigrant families — and its destruction marked the decade for everyone upstream, Auronzo included.

Glasses, and visitors1960s–today

A new trade and an old mountain

What finally gave people a reason to stay was a new trade and an old mountain. By then the old livings were ending — the Argentiera mine closed in 1971, and timber had long since stopped floating down the Piave. The new trade was eyeglasses. Cadore had made spectacles since 1878, when the first Italian eyewear factory opened just down-valley at Calalzo — at first a web of small workshops assembling frames, a successor to the valley's metalworking and an alternative to emigration. From it grew giants: Safilo, descended from that Calalzo factory, and Luxottica, founded in 1961 at Agordo by Leonardo Del Vecchio, who had learned the trade at a firm in Venas di Cadore. The province of Belluno became, and remains, the centre of the world's eyewear industry; Auronzo's own frame-makers, clustered at Cima Gogna, were part of it.

The old mountain was the Tre Cime di Lavaredo. The lake made in 1932, the resort at Misurina, the skiing, and above all the three great towers drew visitors from everywhere, and tourism became the valley's other living. Together, glasses and visitors did what timber and ore once had: they let a person grow up in Auronzo and work there.

Who ruled
The Italian Republic, through the comune of Auronzo.
Living from
Eyewear factories and tourism — the modern successors to timber, mining, and the emigrant trades.
A thread through every era

The valley run by women

The men of Auronzo were forever leaving. They left for the winter forests, for the mines, for the front, and most of all for the emigrant seasons abroad, selling ice cream across Austria, Germany, and the Americas. The work that held the valley together while they were gone was women's.

Women ran the farms and the animals, cut and carried the hay, and worked the high summer pastures — driving the cattle up to the malghe above the valley, where the milk became the butter and cheese that fed the year. When the men were at war, or in a German mine, or behind a gelato counter in Frankfurt, the household, the land, and the children were the woman's to keep. They worked the other trades too: the mine contract of 1886 hired, alongside the men, forty to sixty women to sort the ore by hand; when eyewear came, the assembly benches filled largely with women; when tourism came, so did the kitchens and the hotels.

The valley's story is usually told through its men's trades — the woodcutter, the miner, the soldier, the emigrant. It was as much the story of the women who stayed.

The valley nowtoday

What remains

Two things from the long story have survived into the present. The Regole still exist: reconstituted in 1996, the Magnifiche Regole of Villagrande and Villapiccola again own and manage some fifteen thousand hectares of Auronzo's land in common, exactly as their medieval ancestors did. The oldest layer of all — villages owning a forest together — is also the one still standing.

And the language holds on. Ladin, the mountain Romance speech of Cadore, keeps its rolled plurals (omis, femenes) and words older than Rome (troi, a path; roa, a landslide). To speak it, or to carry a name from these villages, is to belong to a place that was its own for far longer than it has been anyone else's.

In their own words · Ladin, today

troi · roa · omis · femenes

a path · a landslide · men · women

Ladin behaves unlike English here. It was spoken for a thousand years but written late, so there is no "Old Ladin" literature to set beside the modern tongue — only stray words in old Latin documents, then continuous texts from about the 1600s. Standard Italian is younger still in daily life: it became the valley's spoken language only in the twentieth century, through schooling, the army, and television — layered over the Ladin that came first.

The record

Where this history comes from

This account draws on sources in Italian, German, English, and Ladin: the Magnifica Comunità di Cadore and its statutes, local archaeology, museum collections, mineralogical records, and regional histories. Where Auronzo's own record is thin, it leans on the broader history of Cadore and the Belluno mountains — those passages are flagged in the text above.

  • Magnifica Comunità di Cadore — official body and its history of the Cadore statutes (1338) and Regole.
  • Gruppo Archeologico Cadorino (archeocadore.it) — the Monte Calvario sanctuary in Auronzo and the wider archaeology of the valley.
  • Museo Vittorino Cazzetta, Selva di Cadore — the Mesolithic burial at Mondeval.
  • MARC, Museo Archeologico Cadorino, Pieve di Cadore — the Venetic votive bronzes from Lagole (Calalzo).
  • Fogolari & Gambacurta (eds.), Materiali veneti preromani e romani del santuario di Lagole (2001), with A. Marinetti's edition of the inscriptions — the Venetic and Latin (Apollo) dedications.
  • Mindat and regional mining surveys — the Argentiera lead–zinc–silver mine on the Ansiei and the Salafossa mine.
  • Museo degli Zattieri (Codissago, Longarone) and the cìdolo museum (Perarolo) — the timber rafts and the log drives down the Piave.
  • Histories of the Dolomite front, 1915–1918 — Monte Piana and the Tre Cime; the Auronzo civic museum at Palazzo Corte Metto (Great War and mining sections).
  • Accounts of the Operationszone Alpenvorland and the Belluno Resistance — the Garibaldi division "Nino Nannetti" and the province's 1947 gold medal citation.
  • Histories of the Italian eyewear industry — the 1878 Calalzo factory, Safilo, and Luxottica; the Museo dell'Occhiale, Pieve di Cadore.
  • Studies of postwar mountain depopulation and the Vajont disaster (9 October 1963).
  • Studies of Ladin and the Cadorino dialect; accounts of Cadore and Belluno emigration and the gelatieri; work on women's labour in the Belluno mountains (e.g. Vendramini).
  • Patriarchate of Aquileia and the Patria del Friuli — the medieval church-state that held Cadore.

A note on accuracy: dates for the deepest past are approximate and debated; the figure of about 14,000 dead on Monte Piana, and the roughly 1,900 killed at Vajont, are the commonly cited estimates, with some sources giving higher numbers; the fairness of the 1866 plebiscite has itself been contested by some Veneto historians. Much of the 20th-century economic and social story — the eyewear industry, the partisan movement, the boom-years emigration to Turin — is documented at the Cadore and Belluno level and applied to Auronzo, which is flagged in the text; Auronzo's own record is firmest for mining, the 1932 dam, the Great War front, and its population figures. The language specimens are authentic where a local text survives — the Venetic and Roman dedications are real inscriptions from Cadore's own sanctuaries — while the medieval entry shows the standard opening of an imperial diploma rather than the exact wording of the lost 1077 grant; the old alphabets are shown through type and transliteration, not photographs of the originals. Corrections from family members who know the local record are welcome — this is a living page.
